Description of MUSIC

The World Federation of Music Therapy (WFMT) defines music therapy as “the professional use of music and its elements as an intervention in medical, educational, and everyday environments with individuals, groups, families, or communities who seek to optimize their quality of life and improve their physical, social, communicative, emotional, intellectual, and spiritual health and wellbeing.”

Music therapy includes music listening, practice (singing, instrument playing, rythm training…) and improvisational music-making. Its effectiveness in the treatment of psychiatric disorders has been observed in physical recovery, cognitive functioning, and social and emotional rehabilitation. In the context of autism, this is based on the assumption that music is a language, which can stimulate communication, through the request of joint attention, eye contact and turn-taking, and which is accessible to both verbal and non-verbal people. In addition, practices such as musical improvisation can offer a framed context (musical structure), which provides the possibility of some unpredictability, and "measured flexibility". Finally, musical practice is multimodal; it involves three systems: auditory, visual and sensorimotor.
